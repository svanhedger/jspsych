Debriefing Letter

 

Categorization and Cognitive Performance

Principal Investigator: Dr. Irene Cheung (icheung5@huron.uwo.ca)

October 1, 2020

 

Thank you for your participation in this study! Your participation is greatly appreciated.

The purpose of this study was to assess whether experiencing "cute" images of animals would improve cognitive performance, and if so, whether any cognitive benefits were associated with changes in affect. Before the study, you were randomly assigned to a cute condition or a control conditionfor the initial categorization task. The cute condition contained images of animals that were verified to be "impossibly cute" by independent raters. The control condition, in contrast, contained animals that were just "meh." Despite the fact that the categorization task did not require you to make any judgments related to the cuteness of the images, we predicted that exposure to cute animals would temporarily increase positive affect, which then may confer cognitive benefits in the number memory task. To adequately test this prediction, we included a short assessment of affect between the categorization task and the memory task.

We did not make the "cuteness" aspect of the study known to you as you were completing the study. In this sense, the study used deception as a tactic. We used deception because we were concerned that explicit attention to the cuteness of the images may have affected the results. If knowing the true purpose of the study makes you wish to withdraw your data, please contact us and we will try our best to comply with your request.

We hope this research will help inform the cognitive implications of individual internet-browsing behaviour. We all know that cute animal videos rule YouTube; however, this research will help us appreciate whether "compulsive cute consumption" (CCC) has any associated cognitive consequences.

As a reminder of data confidentiality, we did not collect personally identifiable information as a part of this research study. Anonymous study records will be maintained indefinitely. Anonymous study records will be stored on password-protected servers and will also be made available through a secure, online repository (Open Science Framework), allowing other researchers to access the data. The data uploaded to Open Science Framework will use arbitrary alphanumeric strings to differentiate participants. Data that is presented at scientific conferences or in peer-reviewed publications will aggregate data at the group level. While we will do our best to protect your information, there is no guarantee that we will be able to do so. Representatives of Huron's Research Ethics Board may contact you or require access to your study-related records to monitor the conduct of the research.

 

If you have any questions or concerns, please contact the Chair, Huron Research Ethics Board by emailing huronreb@uwo.ca.

Thank you,

 

Stephen Van Hedger, undergraduate researcher
Dr. Irene Cheung, Principal Investigator
